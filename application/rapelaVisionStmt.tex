\section*{Research Vision}

\textit{Mathematics without biology or biology without mathematics are not as
wonderful}.

% Since pursuing my PhD, my research career has been deeply interdisciplinary
% spanning analytical (e.g., mathematics, signal processing, statistics, machine
% learning, computer science) and experimental neuroscience subjects (e.g.,
% statistical analysis of responses of single cells, electrocorticography, Utah
% arrays, Neuropixels recordings, close-loop experimental control). I delight
% when I create a new method to process biological data, but I delight further
% when I can apply it to discover new aspects of brain function.

My \emph{NeuroAI Research Group} will initially focus on
creating advanced statistical methods to characterise neural and behavioral
data, offline and in real time, for short- and long-duration experiments. In
addition, in collaboration with experimental groups, we will apply these
methods, or those created by others, to investigate behavioural and neural
questions.
%
% The research line that my group will follow best aligns with those of
% \href{https://sites.stat.columbia.edu/liam/}{Prof.~Liam Paninski} and
% \href{https://pillowlab.princeton.edu/}{Prof.~Jonathan Pillow}.

This is an exciting time to work on neuroAI. As the variety of
neural datasets being recorded (e.g., high-channel electrophysiology, one- and
two-photon calcium imaging, voltage imaging, EM, transcriptonics, connectomics)
keeps increasing, and their size and complexity become larger and larger due to
more naturalistic and longer experimentation, there is a critical need for
statistical methods that can draw efficient inferences and extract meaning from
these datasets.
%
In addition, as new optogenetic methods are developed to spatio-temporally
manipulate neural activity, we need these inferences in real time, combined
with methods from optimal control, to maximise the efficiency of these
manipulations.
%
Yet, we are currently not seeing an increase in the number of scientists
trained in both statistical machine learning and neurosciences required for the
development of these methods.
%
My research group will attempt to reverse this trend.

\paragraph{Latent variable models}

It is challenging to extract meaning from the large number of spikes generated
by high-channel count recordings probes, like Neuropixels. To address this
problem, my supervisor at the Gatsby Unit, Prof.~Maneesh Sahani, created
Gaussian Process Factor Analysis~\citep[GPFA;][]{yuEtAl09}, a probabilistic
method to extract low-dimensional representations (i.e., latent variables) from
high-dimensional electrophysiological recordings, and recently extended it in
Sparse Variational Gaussian Process Factor Analysis
\citep[svGPFA;][]{dunckerAndSahani18}.
%
At the Unit I have distributed a highly optimized Python implementation of
svGPFA and explored its capabilities to
\href{https://joacorapela.github.io/svGPFA/auto_examples/index.html}{characterize
several high-channel count neural datasets}.

In collaboration with the laboratory of Marcus Stephenson-Jones at the SWC, I
have extracted svGPFA neural latents from striatal Neuropixels recordings in
mice performing a stereotyped poking sequence task~\citep{thompsonEtAl24}. I
then used these neural latents as inputs to a Hidden Markov Model to decode
mice behavioral states with very high accuracy. We are now observing that
patterns of neural latents detected during behavior replay during sleep.
Similar decoding and replay are obtained using latent variables estimated using
linear dynamical systems, instead of svGPFA.
%
If these observations are confirmed, it will be the first time that accurate
decoding is achieved and replay is detected in continuous latent variables of
large neural populations, bypassing the estimation of place fields.

In collaboration with Prof.~Maneesh Sahani, my group will continue developing
svGPFA and will collaborate with experimental groups at NPP and CDB to explore
the potential of latent variable models for position decoding and replay
detection in the hippocampus.

\paragraph{NeuroAI methods for naturalistic, long-duration and continual
experimentation}

At the SWC and Gatsby Unit, we are pioneering a new type of naturalistic,
long-duration and continual experimentation, the Aeon platform, that will
revolutionise neuroscience experimentation~\citep{campagnerEtAl25b}. 

In 2023-2024 I mentored a very talented undergraduate student from NPP,
\href{https://zimoli02.github.io/}{Ms.~Zimo Li}, in a
\href{https://www.simonsfoundation.org/2023/09/26/simons-foundation-announces-next-class-of-neuroscience-undergraduate-research-fellows/}{Simons
Foundation SURFiN
fellowship}, where she performed the first comprehensive statistical analysis
of Aeon data.
%
The achievements of Zimo were outstanding. She processed very complex data,
that at that stage were poorly organised, and skillfully applied sophisticated
statistical models (e.g., linear dynamical systems, hidden Markov models, point
process regression) and produced the first statistical Aeon discoveries, which
were included in the
\href{https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.07.31.664513v1}{Aeon
platform paper}, with Zimo as coauthor. The collaboration with Zimo taught me
that I can do excellent research with students that do not have a solid
mathematical background.

In collaboration with the Aeon team at the Gatsby Unit and SWC, my group will
continue developing statistical methods for the analysis of Aeon data. Due to
the large size of Aeon recordings, conventional batch machine learning (ML)
methods cannot be used to process Aeon data. Aeon requires online methods that
are able to process non-stationary data and are computationally very efficient
to handle very large datasets. A detailed description of my ML data processing
plans for Aeon data appears in a
\href{https://www.gatsby.ucl.ac.uk/~rapela/npp/submittedProposal.pdf}{collaborative
BBSRC/NSF grant we submitted jointly with the Allen Institute for Neural
Dynamics in 2024}.

\paragraph{NeuroAI methods for intelligent experimental control}

Current neuroscience experimentation is mostly performed in open loop with
predefined sets of rules designed by experimenters. More informative
experiments can be designed if they could be controlled in close loop by
intelligent inferences performed on behavioural and neural data streams.

\href{https://bonsai-rx.org/}{Bonsai} is today the most widely used software
for close loop experimental control in neuroscience. In 2022 I realised that
adding ML functionality to Bonsai could make possible a radically new type of
close loop intelligent experimentation, and led the successful application of
a BBSRC proposal
(\href{https://gtr.ukri.org/projects?ref=BB\%2FW019132\%2F1}{BB/W019132/1}) to
build the \href{https://bonsai-rx.org/machinelearning/}{Bonsai.ML package}.

Most current ML methods operate offline, trained and performing inference on
datasets previously recorded. However, Bonsai.ML requires a different type of ML
methods, which are trained and perform inference on live streams, continuously
delivering data in real time. We built this type of ML into Bonsai.ML. It now
contains probabilistic ML methods for real time processing of behavioral (e.g.,
kinematics inference with linear dynamical systems, behavioral state estimation
with hidden Markov models) and neural data (neural latent variables inference
with linear dynamical systems, hippocampal position decoding and replay detection with a
point process state-space model). Please refer to the
\href{https://bonsai-rx.org/machinelearning/}{documentation of
Bonsai.ML}.

We are now integrating large language models (LLMs) into Bonsai. We are using
them to help Bonsai users build correct and optimised workflows using natural
language. Additionally, we are building a Bonsai LLM node to bring the large
semantic knowledge of LLMs into the experimental controls loop.  Please refer
to the \href{https://github.com/joacorapela/os4ls}{Open Source for the Life
Science proposal} we are currently preparing to support these developments.

My group will continue developing real-time probabilistic ML models to enable
intelligent experimental control in Bonsai.

\paragraph{Collaborations}

In 2019 the Gatsby Unit hired me to facilitate collaborations between the Unit
and the SWC.
%
At the SWC experimental scientists collect rich and complex neural and
behavioral datasets. Because often they don't have the analytical skills to best
characterise these recordings, they end up using simple and sub optimal
statistical techniques. I greatly enjoyed collaborating with SWC experimental
scientists finding together good statistical tools to characterize their data.
Below I highlight one of these collaborations.

To explore collaborative opportunities I attended the SWC Data Clubs, where SWC
scientists presented their latest findings. In one data club Ms.~Mitra
Javadzadeh, Ph.D.\ student in Prof.~Hofer's lab, presented her long-range
cortical communications research, where she
was using Linear Discriminant Analysis to model this communication. I proposed
to use linear dynamical systems instead, as the dynamic characteristic of
this model better suited her experimental setup. Mitra shared with me a sample
of her data and asked me to try my suggestion on it. COVID time was ideal for
this analysis, which produced very interesting results. I shared these initial
results with her, her advisor and my supervisor joined the investigation, and
\href{https://proceedings.neurips.cc/paper_files/paper/2021/file/859b755563f548d008f936906a959c8f-Paper.pdf}{Mitra
presented orally our results at CoSyNe 2022}\footnote{Mitra's presentation starts at
2:59:00}. Now, as faculty member at CSHL, Mitra continues this line of
research~\citep{javadzadehEtAl24}.

I find the research of NPP and CDB very interesting and look forward to
collaborate with their investigators on the use of statistical models
to characterize their data. Specifically, at NPP I see opportunities for
collaborations with the lab of Prof.~Bianco on methods to process calcium
imaging data; with Prof.~Bush on methods to process hippocampal data (e.g.,
decoding, forecasting and replay detection), on EEG
data processing, and on methods for seizure detection and prediction; with
Prof.~MacAskill on RL methods supporting context-dependent decision making and
methods to process calcium imaging data;
with Prof.~Ruediger on methods to characterize response properties of visual
cells.
%
At CDB I see opportunities to collaborate with the groups of Prof.~Barry and
Prof.~O'Keefe on methods to process hippocampal data; with Prof.~Baier on
dynamical systems and epilepsy; with Prof.~Cohen on methods for real-time
experimental control.

\section*{Teaching Vision}

I am experienced teaching quantitative subjects to life sciences students.
Please refer to the Teaching section of my CV.

My teaching philosophy has been heavily influenced by my postdoctoral mentor,
Prof.~Wilson Truccolo, Brown University, and aligns closely with the style of
\href{https://mark-kramer.github.io/Case-Studies-Python/intro.html}{Case
Studies in Neural Data Analysis}, by Prof.~Mark Kramer, Boston University.
%
I deliver lectures that are mathematically rigorous, but skip proofs from the
main thread of content. These proofs are provided in appendices.
%
I complement my lectures with many examples, most often in the form of computer
code. These examples help students grasp the utility of the concepts I teach,
even if they don't fully understand their mathematical underpinning.
%
I design my lectures to be multidimensional, so that both naive and expert
students can benefit from them.
%
Please refer to the
\href{https://github.com/joacorapela/gcnuBridging2023/blob/master/probability/lectures/2_foundations/foundations.pdf}{Foundations
of probability  theory} lecture, delivered in the first edition of Gatsby
Unit's Bridging Program in 2023, as a demonstration of my teaching style.

I am very enthusiastic about learning, and from students' feedback I learned
that some of this enthusiasm transpires in my teaching.
